The course is all about a program that lasts for 12 weeks. So, whenever you begin a new program, you should do the 
following:

1. Determine your primary goal whether cutting, bulking, or recomposition. How to choose? If your main goal is to gain more muscles, then you should start a bulking phase. If your main goal is to get shredded so that you clearly show the muscles you already put on, then you should start a cutting phase. Another way to decide is using your body fat percentage. If your body fat percentage is over 20%, cut it down to 10-13% first. If your body fat percentage is 10-13%, then bulk until you reach 17-20%. The reason why it is recommended to bulk from a very low body fat percentage is that, during bulking, you are increasing your muscle mass, but with a slight increase in the body fat percentage as well. Since you may let the bulking phase last a long time so that you gain more and more muscles, you might end the bulking phase being obese and spending more time during the cutting phase to bring your body fat percentage level down. So, starting a bulking phase with a very low body fat percentage means you have more time to gain more and more muscles before the body fat percentage reaches 20%. So, how long should you bulk or cut? There is no right or wrong or "perfect science" that suggests the ideal length of time you should be bulking or cutting. It depends on how much weight you want to gain or lose. During bulking, you can stop when you feel that you reached the muscle mass that you are satisfied to begin revealing. During cutting, stop when you feel that you have revealed enough muscles or you are satisfied with your body fat percentage. Usually, after a cutting phase, your hormones, appetite, and mental state undergo slight adjustments. So, you need a "maintenance" phase for 2-3 weeks to stabilize your hunger and hormone levels before switching to a bulking phase. Maintenance means you are maintaining body weight. Maintenance phase may also be important as, after months of being in a cutting phase, you may be happy with your new physique and may wish to maintain longer before switching to a bulking phase. You may also want to do "reverse dieting" before maintenance. What is it? Read "37. Cut-to-Maintenance Reverse Dieting".

2. Based on your schedule, follow either the 3-, 4-, or 5-day workout guide PDF. Use "FitNotes" app, "Jefit" app, or "Notes" on mobile to track your weightlifting progress (i.e. weight, sets, and reps). Try to write comments whenever needed (e.g. The machine was unavailable this week so I tried another exercise, I was injured this week so I played with a lighter weight, etc). Notice that it doesn't matter whether you are bulking or cutting, the exercises are the same. Also, the target is the same: whether you are bulking or cutting, you need to focus on compound weightlifting (the exercises are already designed so; you don't need to exert effort in knowing compound weightlifting exercises) and try to do progressive overloading, which is adding weights or reps in every exercise, week after week. The only difference between bulking and cutting is that, during bulking, your body has a lot more calories which will allow you to gain strength faster while lifting. On the other hand, during cutting, your body is in a calorie deficit, so it is more difficult to progressively overload due to lower energy levels. So, it is normal that you find a 10-15% reduction in your lifting during cutting compared to during bulking. At best, try to maintain the strength gained while bulking. Should we incorporate cardio during bulking or cutting? Read the cardio sections in both "33. Work-out Routine for Bulking" and "34. Work-out Routine for Cutting". So, cardio is not essential neither during bulking nor during cutting; during bulking, it is good for your health, and during cutting, it expedites fat loss. For a busy guy like me, it seems like I'll never incorporate cardio :) Anyways, do cardio whenever you can. You can find instructional videos for all the trainings in the PDFs in the following link:
https://www.bradnewtonfitness.com/the-ultimate-beginner-guide-push-pull-legs/

3. In the excel sheet named "Training & Measurements Tracking Software" (every time you begin this program (i.e. every 12 weeks), you should download a new excel sheet, do not overwrite old sheets), enter the data at the top (name, current weight, body fat percentage, etc).

4. Weigh yourself daily and record these measurements in the excel sheet under "BODY WEIGHT TRACKER" section. This must be done early at the morning before eating or drinking anything, after you have been to the bathroom, and in your underwear. The excel sheet will use your daily weight-ins to calculate your average weekly weight change. We are not concerned with daily fluctuations in the weight, we care how the average weight changes from week to week.

5. Calculate your daily calories and macronutrients targets based on your primary goal. How to calculate them?
= Calculate TDEE as follows: TDEE = BMR * activity factor:
	- No exercise --> activity factor = 1.2
	- 1-3 days/week training --> activity factor = 1.375
	- 3-5 days/week training --> activity factor = 1.55
	- 6-7 days/week training --> activity factor = 1.725
= The TDEE is the Total Daily Energy Expenditure. So, eating an amount of calories equal to your TDEE will maintain your weight.

- Bulking: since we are bulking, we need to take in more calories than the TDEE. In the course, he says we can take 10% extra calories, but I would say this can be the minimum. So, we can say that the "calorie surplus" can be in the range of 10-20%.
= Total calories = TDEE * (1.1-1.2)
= Protein = (1.76-2.2) * weight(kg) grams
= Fats = 0.661 * weight(kg) grams
= Carbs = [total calories - (protein * 4) - (fats * 9)]/4 grams
= Note: you should be gaining ~226.8-453.59 grams of weight every week. If you are gaining less, then you are not eating enough, so add 100-150 calories to your total calories. If you are gaining more, then you are eating too much, so subtract 100-150 calories from your total calories. The most important thing is to hit your total calories and protein, the rest of the calories can be split between carbs and fats in any possible ratio.
= For weight = 75.6 kg, body fat percentage = 12%, and an activity multiplier of 1.4:
	- TDEE: 2529 calories.
	- Total calories = 2782-3035
	- Protein = 133g-166g ===> 166g
	- Fats = 50g 
	- Carbs = 417g-480g

- Cutting: refer to "Section 4: Goal 1: Cutting (Fat Loss)".

- Recomposition: since we are doing recomposition, we need to take in calories around the TDEE.
= Total calories = TDEE.
= Protein = (1.76-2.2) * weight(kg) grams
= Fats = 0.661 * weight(kg) grams
= Carbs = [total calories - (protein * 4) - (fats * 9)]/4 grams
= Note: your average weekly weight change should be negligible (±0.4 kg weekly change). If you are gaining or losing more than ±0.4 kg per week, then add or drop 150-200 kcal and re-check after two weeks. The most important thing is to hit your total calories and protein, the rest of the calories can be split between carbs and fats in any possible ratio.
= For BMR = 1702 kcal, weight = 71.15 kg, body fat percentage = 13%, and an activity multiplier of 1.4:
 	- TDEE: 2383 calories.
 	- Total calories ~ 2400
 	- Protein = 125g-156g ===> 140g
 	- Fats = 47g
 	- Carbs = 355g

Enter these calculations in the "MyNetDiary" or "MyFitnessPal" app on mobile. Also, enter your target calories for the current week in the excel sheet. After you have gained some experience with managing your calorie and macronutrient intake (e.g. after 1-2 bulk/cut cycles), you may prefer to use mini cuts and mini bulks to shorten the length of your cutting and bulking cycles. What are they? Read "36. Mini Cuts and Mini Bulks".
